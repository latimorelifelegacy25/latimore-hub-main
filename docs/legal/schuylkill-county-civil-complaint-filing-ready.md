# COMPLAINT IN CIVIL ACTION

Plaintiff, Jackson M. Latimore, Sr. (“Plaintiff”), proceeding pro se, files this Complaint in Civil Action against Defendants Assumption BVM School, Diocese of Allentown, and Carol Boyer, individually and in her capacity as Principal, and alleges as follows:

## I. PARTIES

1. Plaintiff Jackson M. Latimore, Sr. is an adult individual residing at 215 N. Broad Mountain Avenue, Frackville, Schuylkill County, Pennsylvania 17931.
2. Defendant Assumption BVM School (“ABVM”) is a private Catholic educational institution operating at 112 South 7th Street, Pottsville, Schuylkill County, Pennsylvania 17901.
3. Defendant Diocese of Allentown (“Diocese”) is a Pennsylvania religious and corporate entity that exercises supervisory, administrative, and policy-making authority over ABVM, its principal, and its staff, including authority relating to student release procedures, custody compliance protocols, and staff training.
4. Defendant Carol Boyer (“Boyer”) was appointed Interim Principal of ABVM on or about February 14, 2025, and assumed the role of confirmed Principal by approximately late March 2025.
5. At all times relevant to this Complaint, Boyer acted individually and within the scope of her employment and agency as Principal of ABVM.
6. Boyer possessed actual authority over student release decisions, transportation determinations, and communications with parents concerning custody-related matters.

## II. JURISDICTION AND VENUE

8. Venue is proper in Schuylkill County pursuant to Pa.R.C.P. 1006 because the events giving rise to this action occurred within Schuylkill County, Pennsylvania.

### A. Custody Background and School Notice

9. Plaintiff is the biological father of Jaclyn C. Latimore, born October 13, 2018, and Jackson M. Latimore, Jr., born September 20, 2019.
10. During the 2025-2026 school year, Jaclyn C. Latimore and Jackson M. Latimore, Jr. were enrolled students at ABVM.

12. On August 27, 2025, following a full custody trial, Judge Hobbs entered a custody order (“Custody Order”) providing for shared legal and physical custody between Plaintiff and Shawna C. Malloy (“Malloy”) on a week-on/week-off basis, with exchanges at the Frackville Police Station.
13. Paragraph 13 of the Custody Order provides: “No relocation of the child shall occur without the consent of every individual having custody rights or without the court’s approval.”
14. The Custody Order identified Malloy’s designated custodial residence as 629 E. Mahanoy Avenue, Girardville, Pennsylvania.
15. The Custody Order authorized Plaintiff to transport the children to and from ABVM and granted Plaintiff rights to information regarding the children’s schooling.
16. A Temporary Protection from Abuse Order in effect during the relevant period also identified Malloy’s address as 629 E. Mahanoy Avenue, Girardville, Pennsylvania 17935.
17. Plaintiff provided ABVM with documentation reflecting Malloy’s court-recognized custodial residence.

## COUNT I — NEGLIGENCE AGAINST ALL DEFENDANTS

133. Plaintiff incorporates by reference Paragraphs 1 through 132 as though fully set forth herein.
134. Defendants owed Plaintiff and the minor children a duty of reasonable care in the supervision, custody, release, transportation, and communication procedures governing the children while they were in ABVM’s care and control.
135. Defendants breached that duty by failing to implement release safeguards adequate to comply with documented court orders on file with the school.
136. Defendants breached that duty by failing to follow Plaintiff’s explicit written directives regarding dismissal and transportation.
137. Defendants breached that duty by relying on Malloy’s representations over court documentation on file with the school.
138. Defendants breached that duty by failing to verify whether Malloy’s residence was court-authorized before releasing the children after receiving written notice of an alleged relocation violation.
139. Defendants breached that duty by failing to contact the identified CYS caseworker, Mindy Garby, after Plaintiff provided her direct contact information for verification purposes.
140. Defendants breached that duty by failing to adequately train and supervise personnel on custody compliance procedures at the classroom-staff level.
141. Defendants breached that duty by failing to prevent the December 16, 2025 release error acknowledged in writing by Mrs. Campion.
142. Defendants breached that duty by breaking Boyer’s express written remedial commitment made to Plaintiff on December 16, 2025.
143. Defendants breached that duty by releasing Jackson, Jr. on January 12, 2026 approximately twenty minutes after Boyer confirmed by telephone that she would place him on the school van.
144. Defendants breached that duty by applying materially inconsistent safety and release practices based on which parent sought access to the children.
145. Defendants knew or should have known that failing to implement reasonable safeguards after repeated written notice, acknowledged errors, available verification resources, and court documentation would foreseeably result in harm to Plaintiff and the children.
146. The foreseeable harm included loss of custodial time, emotional injury, litigation-related expense, and the risk that the children would be placed in an environment later adjudicated unsafe by a court of competent jurisdiction.
147. Defendants’ conduct constituted negligence under Pennsylvania law.
148. As a direct and proximate result of Defendants’ negligence, Plaintiff sustained damages as described herein.
149. **Legal basis:** Under Pennsylvania law, a negligence claim requires: (1) a duty of care owed by the defendant; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a causal connection between the breach and the resulting injury; and (4) actual damages. *Althaus ex rel. Althaus v. Cohen*, 562 Pa. 547, 553, 756 A.2d 1166, 1168 (2000). Pennsylvania courts recognize that schools owe a duty of reasonable care to students in their custody and control, including in the management of student releases and dismissal procedures. *Feleccia v. Lackawanna College*, 215 A.3d 3 (Pa. 2019).
150. The duty here arises from ABVM’s assumption of custody and control over Plaintiff’s minor children during school hours, its own written policies regarding sign-out procedures, and its actual written notice of the controlling custody order.
151. The foregoing allegations satisfy each element of negligence under Pennsylvania law.

## COUNT II — NEGLIGENT SUPERVISION AGAINST DEFENDANT DIOCESE OF ALLENTOWN

152. Plaintiff incorporates by reference Paragraphs 1 through 151 as though fully set forth herein.
153. The Diocese exercised supervisory authority, administrative control, and policy-making authority over ABVM and its principal and staff.
154. The Diocese’s supervisory authority included authority relating to student release procedures, parental custody compliance, and staff training.
155. On January 21, 2026, Plaintiff provided the Diocese with formal written notice of ABVM’s alleged repeated unauthorized releases, ongoing CYS dependency proceedings, Malloy’s alleged eviction from her court-designated residence, and the foreseeable risk to the children.
156. The January 21, 2026 notice placed the Diocese on actual notice of the specific risks and conduct complained of herein.
157. On April 15, 2026, the Diocese, through Dr. St. Pierre, acknowledged receipt of Plaintiff’s formal intent-to-litigate letter and stated that the matter would be referred to diocesan legal counsel.
158. Boyer was copied directly on the April 15, 2026 response.
159. Plaintiff alleges that the Diocese directed no corrective action.
160. Despite possessing actual written notice of foreseeable risks, the Diocese failed to take reasonable corrective action, failed to direct ABVM to implement adequate custody release safeguards, and failed to adequately supervise, train, and monitor Boyer and ABVM staff with respect to custody compliance.
161. The Diocese’s failures constituted negligent supervision under Pennsylvania law.
162. As a direct and proximate result of the Diocese’s negligent supervision, Plaintiff suffered damages as described herein.
163. **Legal basis:** Pennsylvania recognizes a cause of action for negligent supervision where a principal knew or had reason to know that it had the ability to control its agent, and knew or should have known of the necessity and opportunity to exercise that control to prevent harm to a third party. *Dempsey v. Walso Bureau, Inc.*, 431 Pa. 562, 246 A.2d 418 (1968); Restatement (Second) of Torts § 317.
164. A supervisory entity that receives particularized written notice of ongoing harmful conduct by its subordinate and fails to take corrective action may be held liable for the resulting harm.
165. The Diocese’s possession of supervisory, administrative, and policy-making authority over ABVM and its principal, combined with its actual written notice of the specific risks and its failure to act, satisfies the elements of negligent supervision under Pennsylvania law.

## COUNT III — INTERFERENCE WITH CUSTODIAL RIGHTS AGAINST ALL DEFENDANTS

166. Plaintiff incorporates by reference Paragraphs 1 through 165 as though fully set forth herein.
167. Plaintiff possessed legally established custodial rights to his minor children pursuant to the Custody Order entered August 27, 2025.
168. The Custody Order governed school release and transportation issues involving the children.
169. Defendants had actual knowledge of Plaintiff’s custodial rights from the court orders on file, Plaintiff’s pre-school-year written correspondence, Boyer’s written admissions, and written communications involving ABVM classroom staff.
170. Defendants repeatedly released the children to Malloy after receiving notice that she had allegedly violated the Custody Order’s no-relocation provision.
171. Defendants released the children despite Plaintiff’s explicit written objections, Boyer’s written admissions, the January 12, 2026 telephone confirmation followed by immediate non-compliance, and the availability of CYS verification resources.
172. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ conduct substantially interfered with Plaintiff’s legally established custodial rights.
173. Boyer’s January 22, 2026 written response stated that ABVM would continue operating under the week-on/week-off schedule without addressing whether Malloy’s actual residence was court-authorized.
174. Plaintiff alleges that Boyer’s January 22, 2026 response constituted an institutional decision to disregard Plaintiff’s documented custody concerns in favor of a release protocol that failed to address the alleged relocation violation.
175. As a direct and proximate result of Defendants’ interference with Plaintiff’s custodial rights, Plaintiff sustained damages including loss of custodial time, emotional distress, litigation expenses, and other damages to be proven at trial.
176. **Legal basis:** Plaintiff’s custodial rights are established by court order pursuant to 23 Pa.C.S. § 5303, which governs the award and enforcement of custody rights in Pennsylvania. Pennsylvania recognizes a common-law tort claim for intentional or reckless interference with lawfully established custodial rights. See *Rosefield v. Rosefield*, 221 Pa. Super. 69, 289 A.2d 445 (1972); see also Restatement (Second) of Torts § 700.
177. A defendant who knowingly facilitates deprivation of a parent’s court-ordered custodial time, with knowledge of the controlling order and in disregard of explicit written directives from the custodial parent, may be held liable for the resulting loss of custodial time and associated damages.
178. Defendants had actual written knowledge of the Custody Order, received repeated written directives from Plaintiff, acknowledged their own errors in writing, and continued releasing the children to Malloy in violation of the Order. These allegations state a legally cognizable claim for interference with Plaintiff’s custodial rights under Pennsylvania law.

195. **Legal basis for punitive damages:** Punitive damages are available in Pennsylvania where the defendant’s conduct is outrageous because of the defendant’s evil motive or reckless indifference to the rights of others. *Hutchison ex rel.
